---
title: "The Cost of Repeating the Same Checks"
description: "Why manual smoke and sanity testing stopped scaling."
pubDate: 2025-05-21
---

Repetition changes the nature of work.

Smoke and sanity tests are designed to be boring. They exist to confirm that nothing obvious has broken — not to explore new behavior or uncover edge cases.

When run occasionally, manually repeating these checks makes sense.

As they became more frequent, the trade‑offs began to shift.

The issue was not an effort alone. It was consistency.

Manual testing depends on focus. It depends on the person running the test recognizing when something feels slightly off. As repetition increases, that signal becomes harder to detect. Familiarity compresses perception.

What began as a quality exercise slowly turned into a memory exercise.

At that point, automation is no longer about speed. It becomes about removing avoidable variation from work that should be stable by default.

Manual testing still had a role. But it was no longer the right tool for repeatedly confirming known behavior.

Automation wasn’t meant to replace judgment. It was meant to preserve it for the work that actually required it.
