---
title: "Phase 1: Why Recordings Failed"
description: "How my quick wins turned into a mess."
pubDate: 2025-07-01
---

The fastest way to start automating a browser is to record what you already do manually. Most browser automation tools have a recorder that watches your clicks and keystrokes and writes the test code for you. I used it, and it felt like a shortcut that was almost too good.

The first few recordings worked. They replicated my manual steps exactly. I could run them and get a green result in seconds. After years of clicking through forms by hand, watching a script do it felt genuinely satisfying.

That feeling didn't last.

The application I was testing runs inside an iframe. The way it renders means that selectors generated by the recorder were deeply tied to the exact structure of the page at the moment of recording. When the UI changed even slightly — a label adjusted, a button repositioned, a form reloaded in a different order — the recorded test would fail immediately. Not because the behavior was broken, but because the script was looking for something that no longer existed in exactly the same place.

Maintaining those recordings turned into its own job. A minor interface update would break tests across multiple forms. Fixing one test would sometimes reveal another assumption buried three clicks deep. I was spending time I didn't have chasing failures that weren't real failures.

The brittleness wasn't a flaw in the tooling. It was a flaw in the approach. Recordings are a transcript. They capture what happened, not what was intended. And the moment the transcript no longer matches the screen, the whole thing stops working.

I had to decide whether to keep patching recordings or stop and build something that could survive change. The recordings had served a purpose: they made the problem concrete and gave me something to compare against. But they were not the foundation.

I needed to go back and build one.
